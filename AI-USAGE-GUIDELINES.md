# Guidelines on usage of more advanced AI systems (e.g. large language models (LLMs), multi-modal language models. etc) for drafting documents for OASIS CoSAI:

_tl;dr: CoSAI contributions are actions performed by humans, who are responsible for the content of those contributions, based on their signed OASIS iCLA (and eCLA, if applicable). \[Each contributor must confirm whether they are entitled to donate that material under the applicable open source license; OASIS and the CoSAI Project do not separately confirm that.\] Each contributor is responsible for ensuring that all contributions comply with these AI use guidelines, including disclosure of any use of AI in contributions._

## General Stipulations

* Selection of AI systems: CoSAI recommends the use of reputable AI systems (lowering the risk of inadvertently incorporating infringing material).  
* Model constraints: Currently, CoSAI or OASIS are not required to have a contract or financial agreement for using AI systems from specific vendors. However, CoSAI editors should consider employing varying tools to avoid potential fairness concerns among vendors.  
* IP infringement: It is the responsibility of the individual who subscribes/prompts and receives a response from an AI system to confirm they have the right to repost and donate the content to OASIS under our rules.  
* Transparency: CoSAI’s goal will be to maintain transparency throughout the process by documenting substantial use of AI systems whenever possible (e.g., the prompts and the AI system used), and to ensure that all content, regardless of production by human or AI systems, was reviewed and edited by human experts. This helps build trust in the standards development process and ensures accountability.  
* Human-edited content and quality control: CoSAI mandates human-reviewed or \-edited results for any final outputs. A robust quality control process should be in place, involving careful review of the generated content for accuracy, relevance, and alignment with CoSAI's goals and principles. Human experts should scrutinize the output of AI systems to identify any errors, inconsistencies, or potential biases.  
* Iterative refinement: The use of AI systems in drafting standards should be seen as an iterative process, with the generated content serving as a starting point for further refinement and improvement by human experts. Multiple rounds of review and editing may be necessary to ensure the final standards meet the required quality and reliability thresholds.

## Specific Policies

1. ### AI Note-Taking Tools:

   (a) Prohibition. In order to preserve member consent, individual contributors may not introduce or use AI-powered recording, transcription, or summarization tools in CoSAI meetings, calls, or collaborative sessions.(b) OASIS exception. OASIS staff may use AI transcription features built into CoSAI’s official meeting platforms (e.g., Google Meet) to produce official notes and action items. This is a staff-managed practice and is the only sanctioned use of AI note-taking in CoSAI sessions.(c) Use in contributions. If an OASIS-generated meeting summary or transcript is used to prepare a CoSAI contribution, that use should be disclosed under the existing transparency requirements.(d) No substitution for attendance. An AI tool joining a session does not count as a contributor being present and cannot be used to satisfy attendance or quorum requirements.

2. ### Autonomous AI Agents:

   An “Autonomous Agent” is an AI system that can generate or submit content with minimal ongoing human direction (e.g., AI coding agents, research agents, or automated workflow tools). (a) Human accountability required. Any agent-assisted contribution must be reviewed and submitted by a named human contributor who takes personal responsibility for the content. Autonomous Agents may not submit contributions to CoSAI workstreams on their own. (b) IP confirmation. The human contributor must confirm they have reviewed the content and have the right to donate it to OASIS. The human is also responsible for signing the iCLA. Neither can be delegated to the agent. (c) Transparency. Contributors must disclose AI tool use when a significant portion of the contribution is drawn from a tool without meaningful changes. Contributors should disclose other uses of AI where it would be useful context for reviewers. Routine use of AI tools for grammar, spelling, or light language clarification does not require disclosure. These disclosures help CoSAI evaluate the impact of AI tools, develop best practices, and adapt its processes over time. Disclosures should be made where authorship is normally indicated — for example, in a document preamble or submission cover note. The recommended format, consistent with the convention adopted by Fedora, the Linux Kernel, LLVM, OpenTelemetry, and other open source foundations, is an “Assisted-by:” notation. Examples: “Assisted-by: AI Assistant \<ai-assistant@coalitionforsecureai.org”. (d) Vendor fairness. The same vendor fairness guidance that applies to AI drafting tools also applies to Autonomous Agents. (e) Mailing lists. Existing mailing list moderation controls remain in effect and will be used to prevent submissions from AI agents.

3. ### No Substitution for Individual Participation:

   No AI tool or agent can stand in for a human contributor in CoSAI’s governance processes. Specifically: (a) Voting. Only individual contributors may vote on CoSAI matters. A vote submitted or triggered by an AI tool without the contributor’s direct confirmation does not count. (b) Attendance and quorum. An AI tool joining a session on someone’s behalf does not make that person present. Quorum and participation requirements must be met by actual human attendance. (c) IPR commitments. Licensing obligations and IP attestations under OASIS policies must be made personally by the individual contributor and cannot be fulfilled by an AI system.

4. ### No Access to CoSAI Shared Resources for AI Agents that have High-risk, Expansive Access:

   (a) Prohibition. AI agents that have high-risk, expansive access (access to publish, access to private data, and subject to untrusted input) are not permitted to access CoSAI’s Google Drive or other shared document repositories, either directly or on behalf of a contributor. Further, it is not permitted to upload content that originated in the CoSAI Google Drive to an LLM. (b) Rationale. CoSAI’s shared drive contains materials at varying stages of development, including working drafts, informal notes, and preliminary thinking that do not represent final CoSAI positions or decisions. Permitting agent access without restriction creates a risk that non-final content could be misinterpreted, misrepresented, or used out of context. (c) Future exceptions. Any proposed exception to this prohibition — such as agent access limited to published, final documents — must be reviewed and approved by OASIS staff and the CoSAI PGB before being implemented, and must specify the purpose, scope, and access controls in place.  
   For example, it would be permitted to use a personal agent if and only if the agent cannot autonomously publish its outputs publicly.

5. ### Virtual Employees from Member Companies Access to CoSAI Participation or Shared Resources:

   AI agents hosted on system service accounts (i.e. non-personal) that are intended to fulfill the role of an employee or otherwise automate a business function acting as an agent acting *on behalf of the member company* are not permitted to vote, access resources, or otherwise substitute for PBG or TSC attendance or activities. 

   This does not preclude OASIS or CoSAI-hosted or \-created virtual employees.
