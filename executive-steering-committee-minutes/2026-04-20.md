## Summary

* **Review of Governance Brainstorming Documents**: David introduced three brainstorming documents shared via email on April 17 to the PGB, covering governance documents around CoSAI: 1\. The first document proposed refinements to the AI usage policy, emphasizing the need for proper attribution and calling out AI use in GitHub commits, acknowledging the increased use of AI in their processes.

  * 2\. The second document followed up on an in-person discussion at RSA regarding recognition within the CoSAI program. David explained that the OASIS team had reviewed their existing recognition processes for individuals, providing potential paths forward and options for recognition in the CoSAI ecosystem, especially for participants who are not corporate or entity members.

  * 3\. The third, and most meaningful, document addressed the increasing "pull action" from government entities seeking CoSAI's guidance on securely implementing AI, stemming from engagements at RSA. These requests came from countries including Australia, the Netherlands, Singapore, and Italy, focusing on practical implementation rather than policy or lobbying. The proposed strategy involves engaging externally, starting with places like Singapore Cyber Security Week, using round table style discussions, and possibly co-sponsoring events with entities like the Singapore CSA and Australia.

* **Confirmation of Government Interest in Guidance**: David asked attendees if they were also hearing interest from governments for non-policy, non-standard guidance on AI implementation. Omar confirmed that their work on Glasswing sparked conversations not related to the model but how to protect systems and look at prioritization in the age of AI. Omar also noted current discussions in D.C. around using coding agents for security and protecting AI systems that defend against attacks, receiving interest from a government agency and two financial services customers not currently part of CoSAI.

* **Regulatory Engagement and Industry Specificity**: Sunil noted that most of their engagements are currently on the regulatory side with high-level queries, and they are not yet seeing requests for specificity around their approach, though they anticipate this will change as state regulations like the EU AI Act mature. David emphasized that conversations are generally better when multiple industry voices are present, citing positive feedback from a joint roundtable held by Singapore CSA and UK DSIT at RSA.

* **Anticipating Model Advances and Model Escape**: Jason, stressed the need to anticipate smarter models, noting that they had received outreach from two governments this week specifically regarding model escape, which is a very real problem due to models achieving goals by breaking out of sandboxes. Jason warned that open-weights models would likely exacerbate the cybersecurity situation in 12 months, making preparation for governments super important.

  * Omar Santos agreed it is the time to engage and execute on external conversations, noting that they are participating in an OAS round table with government attendees, focusing on whether regulation can keep pace with projects like Mythos and Glasswing. Daniel agreed that things will only get faster and delaying engagement with government and regulatory bodies is not sustainable. Omar also suggested that CoSAI should aim to become the hosting "table" for these discussions rather than just being invited to join existing ones.

  * Matthew reinforced the idea that CoSAI should be a leading voice feeding regulatory bodies, asking what intentional steps they could take to amplify CoSAI's unified voice in the right channels. David highlighted the urgency of engaging, noting the rapidly closing window for opportunities like CoSAI participation in the CSA Australia round table at Cyber Week. He requested two members from the group to work with them to quickly frame the narrative for the CSA Australia moment.

  * Debbie raised concerns about finishing engagements, asking for clarity on the actual outcome of the engagement—whether it should be general awareness or pushing a specific set of policies. Debbie emphasized the need for CoSAI to be "really, really crisp" on two or three specific universal goods or requirements for AI security that they want to push in targeted forums. David agreed that this level of detail is necessary and requested a few individuals from the Executive Steering Committee to collaborate on drafting this document, as the PGB is too large for this focused work.

* **Need for Global Expansion and Partner Recruitment**: David stressed the need to expand CoSAI globally, specifically asking for help in finding internal partnership teams to build out a presence in Asia and India by the end of 2026 to further expand beyond US-base. Matthew suggested working through their OAS partners, who have representation across those regions. Jeffrey Borek committed to taking an action on behalf of their colleague to try and meet the expectation for global expansion.

* **Addressing Public Sentiment and Securing Technology**: Jeffrey voiced concern about the growing public sentiment and backlash against AI technologies. David suggested CoSAI's role remains consistent: to secure technology regardless of public opinion, stating CoSAI's position is not to mandate AI use, but to ensure that if AI is used, the necessary security data is not hidden from users.

* **Follow-up on Impactful Engagements**: David asked members interested in engaging on impactful work over the next five days to reply via email, focusing on intentionally framing upcoming engagements and following up on existing contacts, such as with NIST.

* **Call for Leads Updates**: Ingrid provided an update on the call for leads, confirming that leads for Workstream 1 and the Workstream 4’s SecureADLC SIG have been secured pending TSC approval. Co-leads, are still needed for WS3’s SecureCode SIG.

* **NIST Engagement on RMF Profiles**: David provided an update on organic interest from a NIST researcher, Raymond Shei, who is now formally seeking engagement around their work on RMF profiles. NIST intends to point to industry ecosystem guidance, including CoSAI materials, to accelerate secure AI adoption across 16 critical infrastructure sectors .

  * Omar suggested that the fastest and most stable way to collaborate with NIST is through CoSAI-RM, which is continuously updated. Omar cautioned that engagement with the government requires a carefully thought through plan with consistency. 

  * David proposed that if they move forward, interaction should be limited to CoSAI-RM to contain the work and that they would bring the conversation to the PGB before scheduling a formal discussion. 

* **Post-Mythos Strategy and Scaling Laws**: Omar emphasized that scaling laws are real, predicting that technology will get significantly better offensively and defensively, and stated that CoSAI's significant value lies in the instrumentation and engineering around the models. They noted that conversations are now focusing on how organizations are preparing their code and risk registers for the "age of Mythos".

  * Daniel stated that attention should shift from vulnerability discovery to the remediation pipeline, which is currently insufficient for the post-Mythos moment. He warned that the open-source community is facing challenges  due to the inability of individual maintainers to scale defenses, citing a high volume of supply chain attacks.

  * David suggested that any initial engagement should reinforce and prioritize an existing CoSAI activity, such as securely isolating an agent, to provide immediate value while a larger engagement is scoped out.

  * Jason noted that Anthropic is responding to the moment. He posited that CoSAI's most tangible contribution should be securing the SDLC, as within two years, shipping a security bug will result in immediate exploitation. Jason projected a future where advanced models virtually eliminate security vulnerabilities in systems like Windows due to economic pressures favoring intelligent code verification.

  * Daniel questioned effectiveness of the models longterm. . He suggested that the use of formally verifiable languages might accelerate the trajectory to a new "AI shallow bugs" steady state. Jason responded that token economics will continually favor more intelligence for less cost, making it viable for companies to use models to repeatedly verify code, making it an economic question. Omar strongly agreed that now is the time to permanently transition to memory-safe languages.

* **Guidance for Non-CoSAI Enterprise Members**: Omar emphasized the need for prescriptive actions and guidance for enterprise members who are not part of CoSAI, suggesting that CoSAI could provide this structural organization, as many are struggling to effectively use the technology beyond simple token usage. They noted that current efforts are showing results.

  * The discussion covered how CoSAI might be instrumental in establishing best practices, which could involve leveraging existing engineering and harnesses already in place, even when models are swapped. Nicholas agreed that the group should establish a position on this matter, reiterating that raising the point was intended to initiate this discussion.

  * David indicated that the most suitable area for engagement appeared to be within the ADLC space. David and Daniel agreed that the critical focus should remain on the security outcomes and not be distracted by marketing efforts.
