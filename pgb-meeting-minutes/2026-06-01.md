* **New member introduction:**

* **Upcoming PGB and TSC Co-Chair Elections:**

Attendees were informed that a call for self-nominated candidates for all four leadership seats would be forthcoming.  
* **TSC Co-Chair Expansion Proposal:**  
  * A proposal was introduced to expand the TSC co-chair seats from the current limit of two to a minimum of three. David L. explained that this expansion is critical for scaling operations and facilitating better engagement, while also hoping to increase regional representation outside of the United States. Kelly confirmed that while current governance language is scoped to two, OASIS staff can draft and circulate the necessary changes for a vote once approved.  
  * Regarding the upcoming governance votes, David L. emphasized the need for active participation from PGB members to reach a quorum.   
* **Strategies for Contributor Recognition:**  
  * Jason raised the topic of incentivizing "doers" within the TSC by increasing the visibility and prestige associated with the work. Suggestions included creating formal titles for LinkedIn or resumes and implementing recognition programs like a "Hall of Fame" or podcasts that spotlight specific contributors. David L. and Omar supported these ideas, noting that staff will circulate the recognition ideas document for review and additional PGB input.  
* **AI Usage Policy Update:**   
  * Kelly provided an update on the AI usage policy, which is still in draft form. Key topics under discussion include guidelines for autonomous agents, whether AI agents should have access to shared resources like Google Drive, and requirements for crediting AI-generated work in CoSAI deliverables.  
  * The discussion turned to the security risks of allowing AI agents access to private organizational resources. David L. emphasized the need to establish rules, particularly concerning the prohibition of republishing internal data, while Jason and Daniel identified the "holy trifecta" of vulnerability as the combination of untrusted input, access to private information, and internet connectivity.  
  * Jason and Daniel also addressed the emerging concept of "virtual employees”. They reached a consensus that policy must clearly define attribution and accountability, ensuring that there is always a human or responsible party linked to the actions taken by these agents, even if the tools themselves lack perfect technical accountability.  
* **Premier Dues Pooling and Budget Status:**  
  * David L. reaffirmed the decision to continue pooling dues. In response to Daniel's inquiry about budget utilization, it was noted that there is a remaining gap in spending due to planned initiatives that were not executed, but the organization aims to improve budget deployment in the coming year.  
  * Kelly presented a draft budget totaling just under $350,000, which includes rollover funds and renewed contributions. This figure is expected to rise as more members commit to pooling dues, and the proposal maintains flexibility by allowing OASIS staff and co-chairs to execute within budget buckets to avoid voting fatigue.  
  * The proposed budget includes allocations for several categories: events and small meetups, marketing and content (such as YouTube series and storyboarding), tooling and infrastructure, research and benchmarking, and localization/translation efforts. Additionally, a small contingency fund was proposed to allow chairs to act quickly on short-runway opportunities.  
  * David L. requested that PGB members provide opinionated feedback on the budget document rather than generic comments. He emphasized that member insight is crucial to determining the strategic direction and application of effort, which will dictate how funds are allocated.  
* **External Outreach Strategy:**   
  * The group discussed the surge in inbound requests for engagement from governmental bodies, including those in Australia, Singapore, Italy, and the Netherlands. David L. highlighted that the team is developing a more concrete strategy to guide these interactions, shifting from reactive responses to a structured framework that promotes recognition, influence, and adoption.  
  * The proposed strategy for outreach includes hosting small, closed roundtable-style events under Chatham House rules, involving 20 to 50 participants. This approach aims to provide guidance and foster relationships with governmental entities and other key stakeholders without needing to scale to massive industry events.  
  * Omar and David L. stressed the need to move quickly, suggesting that the team should not wait for the next major industry event but rather create their own tables. A goal was set to gather significant feedback on the draft outreach strategy within five business days to accelerate progress.  
* **Engagement of an External Consultant:**   
  * To fill the gap where OASIS infrastructure and member time cannot meet strategic needs, leadership proposed hiring a part-time consultant to drive external strategy and engagement. Omar described the ideal candidate as a "linker" who is already plugged into the ecosystem, capable of organizing stakeholders, driving conversations and events without requiring excessive guidance.  
  * Sarah and David L. discussed that the ideal consultant should have deep relationships with CISOs, security leads, and government officials, and the ability to influence strategic budget decisions. The group acknowledged that if the right person cannot be found, it may not be worthwhile to proceed with the hire.  
  * The participants debated whether the strategy role should be a single individual or a team, noting that a team might be necessary to initiate the strategy before handing execution to a single person. It was agreed that the job description and strategy document require further refinement before being shared with the group for final review.  
* **Proposal for Secure Code Scanning:**  
  * David L. introduced a speculative proposal regarding "secure code scanning," where members could scan open-source software against non-public, foundational models. The goal is to allow organizations to scan code against advanced models without risking "distillation attacks" or leaking proprietary model information to competitors.  
  * Jason and Daniel discussed the feasibility of this proposal, suggesting that confidential computing could be used to host and secure the scanning process. This would allow companies to scan code without the host entity having access to the source code or the model, thereby protecting intellectual property while solving a critical infrastructure problem for organizations with limited technical talent.  
  * Daniel emphasized that achieving transparency in models requires patterns such as attested confidential computing. He argued this approach is essential for training on sensitive data where organizations are not legally permitted to hold the data. This method creates a "rendezvous" point where two parties navigate trust relationships to produce a shared output.  
  * David L.and Daniel discussed the need to establish "invariants" as the minimum bar for execution, rather than relying solely on open-source project structures. David L. notes that even within confidential computing, risks remain, such as data bleeding through side-channel attacks if a user performs infinite testing or scanning. He explored the distinction between one-way trust and bilateral trust, suggesting that the latter represents a larger opportunity.  
  * Omar advocated for the creation of a reference architecture to demonstrate team alignment and provide clear guidance to the industry. He suggested that the group should coordinate with existing bodies to provide consistent direction to organizations attempting to solve similar problems.  
  * David L. proposed engaging the Action Institute at UC Santa Barbara to research invariants and academic constraints. He emphasized that any proposed architectural design must provide tangible value to foundational model providers or the project will lack impact. He also acknowledged that this effort may require CoSAI to adapt its structure to facilitate this work.  
* **Interoperability and Next Steps:**   
  * David L. suggests holding an "interop day" event to bring together researchers, confidential GPU experts, and CoSAI members to foster live collaboration. He also requested that foundational model providers decide within the next few days if they want to pursue this path and identify necessary legal requirements for their organizations. David L. plans to distribute a document for review and asks participants to coordinate further discussions to move the initiative forward.
